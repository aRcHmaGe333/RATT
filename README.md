## The Concept

RATT is a system that continuously takes in items for review. Multiple large language models (LLM) then examine and describe/evaluate it independently, from different angles (points of view) and through different personalities. With multiple passes each (first by utilizing their default, then with personality and standpoint altered, -||- altered further, et cetera), they provide an array of 'reactions' to the item. Personalities/angles can be randomized each time to put different models in different roles each time. The system generates a fusion/synthesis by comparing their reviews: where they align, where they split, what grounds each perspective and what stood out as odd, perhaps. The LL models then respond to each other's reviews and discuss about possible improvements for a few rounds, which enables them to produce updated versions,  not necessarily better versions. It all stays documented, as a council of minds, nothing is irreversibly lost because the journey itself is a part of the knowledge gained, opinion adjusted, person informed or disinformed. (Be vigilant against the normalized lies!)  

All reviews stay public, timestamped, and versioned. When new information arrives, reviews update. Historical versions remain archived.
Users get to discuss and point out things. Perhaps there can be a track record which notes various parameters and traits of the reviewers, their overall ability, the language they use (writing skill), etc. 

---

## Why It Works

RATT puts multiple frameworks to the same task at the same time. It shows where thinking holds, where it fractures or excels. 
Evidence is attached to every claim. Reviewer reliability becomes measurable through track record. Opinions surface next to assessments, they are challenged and altered, but never closed! Beliefs, dogma, evidence, trends, weights, it will all become apparent. Due to the tendency for troll-spam of the internet as an environment (in connection with opinionated discussions), it might be necessary to validate identity (in the interest of decent behaviour and minimized disturbances). On the other hand, this can benefit the user because their contributions remain visible and evident. Time will tell how this plays out.

---

## Core Principles

- Public, timestamped, and versioned reviews — historical versions remain accessible and discussions can be re-opened.
- Multiplicity: independent perspectives, not a single authority.
- Transparency: reasoning, evidence, and research chains are visible.
- Autonomy: RATT operates continuously (feeds, discovery, nominations, and optional submissions).

---

## What Gets Reviewed

Anything that can be assessed: code, research, proposals, designs, writing, art, services, other public or discoverable artifacts. Some things that were never reviewed before. They are be added via automated feeds, third-party nomination, or optional direct submission; the system also discovers items autonomously. Users can build their own streams of reviews which are focused on their area of interest/expertise and by doing this, generate informative, useful and/or entertaining content. 
Items arrive through submission, nomination, automated feeds, discovery protocols. 

---

## How It Works

An item enters the queue.
Multiple language models review it independently, each operating from a specific stance: skeptic, advocate, historian, ethicist, pragmatist, rigorist, aesthete.
Research agents verify flagged claims and collect supporting material.
The system maps where reviews converge and diverge. 
A moderator agent/protocol allows for consensus, productive disagreement, and evidence gaps; reviewers see one another's work and generate structured dialogue where it's appropriate.
5. All outputs are archived, searchable, and updateable when new information arrives.
All assessments are versioned, archived, indexed.
Reviews evolve as new information surfaces.
Perhaps even the users can join as reviewers.

---

## Available To The Audience/Users

Each reviewed item includes:
- All independent reviews
- Alignment and conflict between reviewers
- Evidence supporting each claim
- Confidence levels per reviewer
